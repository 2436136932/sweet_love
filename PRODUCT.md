# Product

## Register

product

## Users

Sweet Love is for two people in a committed relationship who want a private, shared place for everyday memories and small coordination. They use it on mobile first, often in short moments: checking what the other person left, adding a photo, writing a diary note, deciding what to eat, updating a tiny status, or recording something worth remembering.

The product serves both partners equally. It should feel personal and intimate without making either person work hard to understand the interface. The main job is not to impress a visitor; it is to help a couple keep a lightweight shared home that survives refreshes, device switches, and busy days.

## Product Purpose

Sweet Love brings anniversaries, diaries, albums, messages, 100 shared things, daily ratings, period care, ordering, kitchen planning, and AI-assisted drafts into one private couple space.

Success means users can open the app and quickly know: what changed, what needs attention, what can be recorded now, and what can be done together next. AI features must behave like draft helpers, never like autonomous actors: they suggest wording, ideas, care messages, and recipe drafts, but the user always confirms before anything is saved or sent.

## Brand Personality

Warm, private, and light.

The voice should feel affectionate and calm, like a small shared room rather than a public social feed. It can be sweet, but it should not become childish. It can be playful, but not noisy. It should make both people feel gently seen, not managed.

## Anti-references

- Full-screen pink capsule overload: too many saturated pills, rounded blocks, and decorative badges competing for attention.
- Crowded mobile cards where names, status text, and action buttons squeeze each other into broken layouts.
- Dating-app or social-feed energy: public, performative, swipey, or engagement-chasing.
- Cold admin-tool UI that strips away photos, emotion, and intimacy.
- Medical or diagnostic UI for period features. Period health and trying-to-conceive mode are for shared records, reference predictions, trends, and care reminders only; they must not make medical, contraception, pregnancy-probability, or fertility-treatment claims.
- AI that appears to act on behalf of the user without confirmation.

## Design Principles

1. **Private room, not public stage.** The product should feel like a quiet shared space for two people. Photos and personal details are meaningful, but the interface should not perform for outsiders.
2. **Mobile comfort first.** Every page must work inside the narrow phone shell before it works anywhere else. Text cannot wrap into one-character columns, buttons cannot crush content, and important actions must stay reachable.
3. **Sweetness with restraint.** Use romance as tone and small moments, not as endless pink decoration. Accent color should guide attention, not coat every surface.
4. **Draft before action.** AI output, generated recipes, care suggestions, and message copy are suggestions until the user explicitly adopts them.
5. **Shared but understandable.** Data that belongs to the couple should be visibly shared, while personal controls such as "my status" should still feel clearly owned.

## Accessibility & Inclusion

Target WCAG AA contrast for body text and controls. Keep touch targets at least 44px where possible. Support reduced motion by ensuring animations are short, state-driven, and non-blocking. Avoid relying on color alone for important states. Period assistant copy must stay non-diagnostic and avoid pregnancy probability, safe-period advice, treatment claims, or medical certainty.
